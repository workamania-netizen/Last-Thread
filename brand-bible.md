# Last Thread Co. — Brand Bible

## Brand thesis
For the men who wear things out.

## What we sell
Curated gifts for men. Stage 1: affiliate-only (Amazon primarily). Stage 2 
(later): branded Printify merch redirects alongside affiliate items.

## Audience
- Primary (60-65%): women buying gifts for husbands, fathers, sons, brothers
- Secondary (25-30%): men buying for themselves or for other men
- Age: 35-65, mostly suburban/exurban, middle-class household income
- Cultural affinity: "salt of the earth" values — practical, frugal, loyal 
  to things that work, allergic to wastefulness and pretense

## Brand voice rules
- Observational, dry, warm
- Editorial — like a magazine, not a product page
- Slightly humorous, never sarcastic at the audience's expense
- Specific over abstract — "the hoodie he's had for 17 years" beats 
  "his favorite clothes"
- Short sentences. Active voice. Confident.
- Sentence case for all UI and headlines. ALL CAPS only for small 
  label-style tags.
- Never use: "elevate," "curated experience," "thoughtfully designed," 
  "luxury," "premium," "lifestyle," any startup-speak or marketing-speak
- Always use: real-world specifics, gentle wryness, warmth

## Sample copy in voice

Product blurbs:
- "The kind of socks he'll wear until they have holes in them. Then you 
  can throw the old ones out without him noticing."
- "He's been carrying the same wallet since the second Bush administration. 
  It's time."
- "Cast iron lasts longer than most marriages. Get him the one his 
  grandkids will inherit."

Brand pitch (homepage):
- "Most men don't buy themselves nice things. They mean to. They never 
  get around to it. They wear the same hoodie until it's a relic. They 
  drive the same truck until it's a member of the family. They use the 
  screwdriver with the cracked handle because it still works."
- "Last Thread Co. is for that man. And for the people who love him 
  enough to finally upgrade his socks."

## Tagline
"For the men who wear things out."

## Brand mark
Wordmark: LAST THREAD CO. in Fraunces, letterspaced, often centered.
Conversational shorthand: Last Thread.

## Design system
- **Background:** #f5f1ea (warm cream)
- **Text primary:** #1c1c1a (warm near-black)
- **Text secondary:** #5a544a (warm gray)
- **Accent:** #a44a2a (rust copper) — buttons, links, hover
- **Accent dark:** #82391f — hover/pressed states
- **Secondary accent:** #5a6b4d (faded olive) — tags, labels
- **Border:** #d4c8b8 (soft tan)
- **Surface:** #fbf8f2 (slightly lighter cream for cards)

## Typography
- Headlines: Fraunces (Google Font), weight 500 for most, 600 for hero
- Body: Inter (Google Font), weight 400 for body, 500 for emphasis
- Line height: 1.7 for paragraphs
- No third font family. For small labels, use Inter uppercase letterspaced.

## Imagery rules
- Always contextual — products in use, on surfaces, in environments. Never 
  isolated white-background catalog shots.
- Warm color grading. Afternoon-light feel.
- No model faces on the homepage hero. The man in the brand is invisible 
  so every man can be him. Hero imagery is environmental (cabin, workshop, 
  fireplace, recliner) or product-focused (leather wallet on wood, worn 
  jacket on a chair).
- For placeholders during build, use Unsplash URLs matching this aesthetic.

## Aesthetic reference
Huckberry (huckberry.com) is the closest analogue. Editorial, masculine, 
warm. Big lifestyle photography. Generous whitespace. Serif headlines, 
clean sans-serif body. Feels like a magazine that sells things.

## What this brand is NOT
- Not feminine-styled for the gift-buyer (kills conversion with female 
  buyers who need to feel confident the man will like it)
- Not corporate or "premium" (we sell socks, not handbags)
- Not streetwear or hypebeast (audience is 35-65, not 22-30)
- Not rustic-themed or country-store kitsch (modern, considered, warm)
- Not faith-forward in branding (founder is a person of faith, but the 
  brand speaks to everyone — Christian values like loyalty, stewardship, 
  honoring the unsung are woven in implicitly, never preached)

## Audience design principle
The primary buyer is a woman buying a gift for a man. She is not buying 
for herself. She validates the purchase by asking "would he find this 
cool / wear this / use this?" The site must feel like a place a man would 
shop, designed welcomingly enough that a woman shops there for him. Not 
the other way around.

This is exactly how Huckberry, Bespoke Post, and Filson succeed at 
cross-gender gift conversion.
